That stopped me. We've been told AI is the great equaliser — the thing that lets a 3-person startup move like a 300-person company. But if Microsoft, with a $3 trillion market cap, can't sustain the cost of AI at scale… what does that mean for everyone else?

I sat with that for a while. And the more I thought about it, the more it felt less like an AI story and more like a symptom of something bigger.

Then I Remembered the J-Curve

I've been going through some startup masterclasses recently, and one concept kept coming back to me — the J-Curve. The idea that most startups don't die because their idea was bad. They die in the dip — the valley between early momentum and sustainable scale, where costs spike, complexity multiplies, and "this is working" quietly turns into "we can't keep this up."

The Microsoft AI story is a perfect J-Curve moment. The tool worked. Adoption shot up. Costs followed. And right when things should have stabilised, the math fell apart.

And I got curious. Is this just an AI thing? Or is the same valley showing up everywhere — healthcare, immigration, SMB operations — where the gap between "this exists" and "this actually works at scale" is where companies quietly go to die?

So I went looking.

How I Looked — And What I Borrowed

I didn't want to just collect complaints. Anyone with a Reddit account can do that. I wanted a way to actually rank these problems — which ones were urgent, big, and genuinely unsolved versus which ones just felt loud.

That's when I came across a framework called Fix My Itch — something Razorpay built brilliantly for India. They scraped Reddit, X, and forums for real problems, then scored each one on severity, frequency, and market size. The "Itch Score." It's such a clean idea, and as far as I can tell, nobody has applied it to the US market yet.

So I borrowed it. Three inputs, simple math:

Gap Score = TAM + Frequency + Whitespace
Adapted from Razorpay's Fix My Itch · Max score: 30
TAM (1–10): How big is the market if solved? Frequency (1–10): How often is this complained about? Whitespace (1–10): How weak are existing solutions?

Frequency comes from Reddit threads, Hacker News, G2 reviews, LinkedIn — wherever people actually vent. Whitespace asks: even if a solution technically exists, does it leave most of the friction in place?

What I Noticed Across All Five

Here's the thing that surprised me most: none of these gaps are about missing technology. The AI tools exist. The healthcare mandate exists. The immigration system exists. The enterprise platforms exist.

What's missing in every case is the layer that makes the existing thing usable — at the right price, at the right scale, for the right kind of company. Microsoft has AI but no way to control what it costs. Doctors have software but no way to escape the paperwork. Startups have tools but the ones built for them assume they're either enormous or tiny — never the in-between.

Every gap I found sits in the same valley — the one between "this exists" and "this actually works for me."

That's the J-Curve at industry scale. And it's where I think the next interesting wave of companies will get built.

Where I'm Going With This

This is just one afternoon of curiosity turned into something. I went down a rabbit hole, found a framework I loved, applied it to a different market, and noticed a pattern I genuinely didn't expect.
